Annotated: Generalised Scalar Product for Kerr QNMs



> Purpose of this notebook: Develop and test a generalised scalar product
> that extends beyond a single QNM frequency, allowing overlaps between eigenfunctions
> belonging to different QNM systems (e.g. different overtone numbers). This directly
> supports the paper arXiv:2312.17678 'A radial scalar product for Kerr quasinormal modes'.
 

Develop and test code for generalized scalar products



This product is based on the following ideas:
* Consider the confluent Heun equation
$ (\mathrm{C0}+\mathrm{C1}(1-\xi))f(\xi) + [\mathrm{C2} + \mathrm{C3} (1-\xi) + \mathrm{C4} (1-\xi)^2]f'(\xi) + \xi (1-\xi)^2 f''(\xi) = 0 $
* This can be transformed into a manifestly self adjoint form by letting $f(\xi)= \eta(\xi) y(\xi)$
* Where $\eta(\xi) = e^{-\frac{\text{C2}}{2 (1-\xi )}} (1-\xi )^{\frac{1}{2} (\text{C2}+\text{C3}+2)} \xi ^{\frac{1}{2} (-\text{C2}-\text{C3}-\text{C4}+1)}$
* We take $f(\xi)$ to be the Frobenius solution about $\xi=0$. So it is appropriate to consider the vector space populated by $y(\xi)=\eta(\xi)^{-1} f(\xi)$.
* Scalar products between solutions $y_1(\xi)$ and $y_2(\xi)$ have weight $1$, and so the definition of the scalar product does not change if the coefficients, $Cs$, are changed due to e.g. different ferquency parameter or BH spin. Let the coefficients for $y_2$ be $Ds$.
* Then the effective weight for the definition of generalised monomial moments is $e^{\frac{\text{C2}+\text{D2}}{2-2 \xi }} (1-\xi )^{\frac{1}{2} (-\text{C2}-\text{C3}-\text{D2}-\text{D3}-4)} \xi ^{\frac{1}{2} (\text{C2}+\text{C3}+\text{C4}+\text{D2}+\text{D3}+\text{D4}-2)}$.
* That is, we redefine the scalar product to have unit weight. This moves information from the weight function to the dependent vaiable. However, the schematic structure of monomial moments is unchanged. We use this fact to define a generalised product in the space of $f(\xi)$ whose effective weight is $e^{\frac{\text{C2}+\text{D2}}{2-2 \xi }} (1-\xi )^{\frac{1}{2} (-\text{C2}-\text{C3}-\text{D2}-\text{D3}-4)} \xi ^{\frac{1}{2} (\text{C2}+\text{C3}+\text{C4}+\text{D2}+\text{D3}+\text{D4}-2)}$
 

What the opening theory block tells us



The markdown header sets out the entire mathematical foundation for this notebook in bullet form:

1. The confluent Heun equation is the ODE that governs Teukolsky's radial problem.
The equation has five coefficients C0–C4 that encode the QNM frequency and black-hole spin.

2. Self-adjoint form — by substituting f(ξ) = η(ξ)·y(ξ) with a specific integrating factor
η(ξ), the equation becomes manifestly self-adjoint. Scalar products between the y functions
have unit weight, which is the key algebraic simplification.

3. Generalised product — because the weight is 1 in the y picture, we can define a scalar
product between solutions from two different physical systems (different C_s and D_s).
The effective weight in the original f picture involves the combined parameters of both
systems. This is the 'generalised' product of the notebook title.

Why does this matter? A scalar product between eigenfunctions from different overtone systems
is precisely the tool needed to ask: *can one overtone's eigenfunctions be expanded in terms of
another overtone's basis?* That is the spectral-decomposition question motivating the paper.

Cell 6 — Inspect the mp_generalized_prod docstring



`python
?rnspec_helper.mp_generalized_prod
`

The docstring (visible in the output) reveals:

- Signature: mp_generalized_prod(this, n1, n2, that=None) — takes two eigenfunction
index positions and optionally a second rnspec_helper object (that).

- Self-consistent — when that=None (or that=this), it computes the standard
scalar product within a single QNM system. When that is a different object,
it computes the generalised cross-system product.

- Moment recursion — the method computes the required monomial moments on the fly
using a recursion relation, which is much faster than calling gamma and hypU
(confluent hypergeometric functions) individually for each moment.

This is the core computational kernel of the entire notebook.

Cell 7 — Compute the self-overlap matrix G (the key orthogonality test)



`python
G = matrix(len(foo1.mp_eigen_vectors), dtype=mpc) # complex matrix
for j in foo1.order_space:
for k in foo1.order_space:
G[j,k] = foo1.mp_generalized_prod(j, k) # ⟨ψ_j, ψ_k⟩
`

This double loop computes every pairwise inner product between the eigenfunctions
of the n=0 system. If the scalar product is correctly defined and the eigenfunctions are
genuinely orthogonal under it, then G should be diagonal (ideally the identity matrix).

What the indices mean: The inputs j and k are not the eigenfunctions themselves
but their index positions in foo1.mp_eigen_vector_monomial_coeff_dict — a dictionary
that stores each eigenfunction as its coefficient vector in the monomial basis.

The commented-out line shows the normalised version:
`python
G[j,k] = foo1.mp_generalized_prod(j,k) / sqrt(prod(j,j) * prod(k,k))
`
This would give the correlation matrix (diagonal entries = 1) rather than the Gram matrix.
The un-normalised version is used here so the diagonal values reveal the norms of the eigenfunctions.

Cell 13 — Compute the cross-system mixing matrix G2 (the core new result)



`python
for j in range(len(foo2.mp_eigen_vectors)):
for k in range(len(foo2.mp_eigen_vectors)):
G2[j,k] = foo1.mp_generalized_prod(j, k, that=foo2) # ⟨ψ_j^(0), ψ_k^(1)⟩
`

This is the most important cell in the notebook.

The that=foo2 argument changes the computation from a self-overlap to a
cross-system overlap: each matrix element G2[j,k] is the scalar product
between the j-th eigenfunction of the n=0 system and the k-th eigenfunction
of the n=1 system:

> G2[j,k] = ⟨ψⱼ^(n=0), ψₖ^(n=1)⟩

Unlike G (which should be diagonal), G2 is not expected to be diagonal —
it is a mixing matrix that encodes how the two overtone systems overlap.

Physical significance: If one wanted to expand a wavefunction built from n=0
eigenfunctions in terms of n=1 eigenfunctions (or vice versa), G2 is exactly
the object needed to compute the expansion coefficients.

Paper link: This directly implements the 'generalised product' discussed in
arXiv:2312.17678 and is the numerical evidence that the scalar product can be
meaningfully extended across different QNM frequency sectors.
